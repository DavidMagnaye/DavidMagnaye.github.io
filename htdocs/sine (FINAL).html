SINES

DEFINITION

SINE

NOUN

-a trigonometric function that for an acute angle is the ratio between the leg opposite the angle when it is considered part of a right triangle and the hypotenuse (Merriam-Webster)

SINE FORMULA

Given one right triangle and an acute angle θ, sin θ = opposite ⁄hypotenuse. This is part of the famous mnemonic "SOH-CAH-TOA" students use to memorize trigonometric equations. SOH-CAH-TOA means Sine = Opposite/Hypotenuse, Cosine = Adjacent/Hypotenuse, Tangent = Opposite/Adjacent.



For example, sin(30o)   =   a ⁄2a   =   3 ⁄ 6   =   1 ⁄2.

However, sin(0°), sin(90°), sin(180°), sin(270°) are special values because the opposite sides of each angle are congruent to the hypotenuses.

NOTE: The sine of an angle is the same no matter how big or how small the triangle is.

SINE in the UNIT CIRCLE


The Unit Circle is a circle in the Cartesian Plane with center at (0,0) and with a radius of 1 unit. Interestingly enough, if you form a right triangle with two vertices at the x-axis and one vertex on the Unit Circle, the value of the sine of that angle is the y-coordinate of the vertex at the circle .

Here we can see that for Quadrant I (0 to 90 degrees) and Quadrant II (90 to 180 degrees), the values of sines are positive while for Quadrant III (180 to 270 degrees) and Quadrant IV (270 to 360 degrees), the values for the sine is negative. This is because the y-coordinates of points in Quadrants I and II are positive while the y-coordinates of points in Quadrants III and IV are negative.

It is also observable that sin(0°) = 0, sin(90°) = 1, sin(180°) = 0, sin(270°) = -1. The sine values of each angle are still equal to the y-coordinates of their respective points.



A more general illustration is an arbitrary circle. Given an arbitrary circle centered at (0,0) with radius r on the Cartesian Plane which passes through P(x,y) at an angle θ, sinθ = y ⁄ r.

SINES OF NEGATIVE ANGLES

Given an angle θ, sin(-θ) = -sin(θ).

This is because when you take -θ, the angle is reflected along the x-axis, meaning it will have the negative y-coordinate of the initial angle θ. A negative y-coordinate would mean a negative sine value.

For instance, sin(-30°) = -(√3/2) = -√3/2 = -sin(30°). This is also true for negative angles themselves. For example, sin(30°) = (√3/2) = -(-√3/2) = -sin(-30°).

SINES OF ANGLES GREATER THAN 360°

Given an angle θ ≥ 360° , sin(θ) = sin(θ mod 360°) where θ mod 360° is the remainder when θ is divided by a full-circle revolution or 360°.

This is because when you take θ, there is a reference angle that is coterminal with θ. These reference angles are not affected by a full revolution because full revolutions would return an angle to its original position.

For instance, sin(405°) = sin(405°-360°) = sin(45°) = sin(45°-360°) = sin(-315°) = √2/2.



THE SINE CURVE

The graph above is the graph of y = sinθ. It is a continuous and oscillating graph. There are two main things to notice here: amplitude, and period. Since y = sinθ is the default function for all sine functions, the properties of its graph also have default values.

The amplitude of a sine graph is the height from the center line to the crest or trough (or in other words, the total height of the graph divided by 2). The period of the sine graph is the length of one cycle of the curve. The function above has an amplitude of 1 and a period of 2π.

In general, the equation of a sine function is y = A sin(B(θ+c)) + D. The amplitude of the sine function is equal to A. The period is equal to 2π/B. Two other properties, vertical shift and phase shift, determine the length of vertical shift and horizontal shift from the original graph, respectively.
